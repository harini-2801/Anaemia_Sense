ℹ️ Medical Context

Anemia is characterized by a decrease in the total amount of red blood cells (RBCs) or hemoglobin in the blood, resulting in a reduced ability of the blood to carry oxygen. Correctly inputting all laboratory parameters allows the model to screen for abnormalities and guide diagnostic subtypes.

🔬 Blood Sizing & Classification

Microcytic (MCV < 80 fL): Small RBC size, commonly caused by iron deficiency, thalassemia, or lead poisoning.

Macrocytic (MCV > 100 fL): Enlarged RBC size, commonly caused by folate or Vitamin B12 deficiency (pernicious anemia).

Normocytic (MCV 80–100 fL): Normal cell sizing, typical of acute blood loss, hemolysis, or chronic diseases.

🧬 Understanding Anemic Symptoms

Anemia results in tissues not receiving sufficient oxygen. Symptoms vary depending on the severity and rate of onset. Watch out for these primary warning signs:

💤 Fatigue & Weakness

The most common symptom, caused by lack of oxygen delivered to skeletal muscles and brain tissue.

❄️ Cold Hands & Feet

Vasoconstriction pulls blood away from extremities to prioritize oxygen flow to vital organs.

🫁 Shortness of Breath

The body hyperventilates to draw in more oxygen as hemoglobin capacity is diminished.

🧠 Dizziness & Headache

Reduced blood pressure and brain oxygenation cause headaches, lightheadedness, or fainting.

🔬 Blood Index Chart

Hemoglobin (Hb): Male: 13.8–17.2 g/dL | Female: 12.1–15.1 g/dL. Low values define anemia.

Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV): 80–100 fL. Indicates average red blood cell size.

Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin (MCH): 27–33 pg. Average weight of Hb inside an RBC.

MCHC: 32–36 g/dL. Average concentration of Hb inside cells.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What parameters are required to run this model? ▼
The system requires five clinical indexes: Biological Gender, Hemoglobin (total oxygen-carrying protein), Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV - cell size), Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin (MCH - hemoglobin mass per cell), and MCHC (hemoglobin concentration per cell). These values are standard components of a Complete Blood Count (CBC) panel.
How accurate is the machine learning classifier? ▼
The predictive engine runs a Gradient Boosting Classifier. It was evaluated on balanced clinical registries and achieved a cross-validated testing accuracy score of 98.9%. This makes it a highly sensitive tool for preliminary screening.
Can all values be high and still output "No Anemia"? ▼
Yes. Anemia is medically defined as a deficiency of red blood cells or low hemoglobin levels. If your blood indexes are abnormally high, you do not have anemia (elevated values could indicate conditions like polycythemia or dehydration). The model will output "No Anemia" correctly. However, our dashboard now alerts you if your indexes are out-of-bounds high, prompting a physiological warning.
Is this a substitute for a medical diagnosis? ▼
No. This application is an educational screening tool using machine learning. It is not a substitute for a professional clinical diagnosis by a doctor or hematologist. Always consult with a qualified physician regarding laboratory results.
